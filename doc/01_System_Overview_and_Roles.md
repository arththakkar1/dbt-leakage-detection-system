# DBT Leakage Detection System: Overview and User Roles

## 1. System Overview

The Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) Leakage Detection System is an advanced, real-time transaction monitoring and anomaly detection platform designed specifically for the state of Gujarat. The core objective of the system is to identify, flag, and mitigate fraudulent or erroneous disbursements of welfare funds across multiple state-sponsored schemes before the next payment cycle occurs.

### 1.1 Problem Statement Context
Currently, the state disburses funds across numerous welfare schemes. However, inherent vulnerabilities in the data and distribution channels lead to significant financial leakages. These known failure modes include:
*   Disbursements to ineligible beneficiaries or individuals who are deceased.
*   Intermediaries or middlemen intercepting funds by routing transfers to non-beneficiary accounts.
*   Accumulation of unwithdrawn funds in beneficiary accounts, often indicating that the legitimate beneficiary is unaware of the transfer or the account is dormant.
*   Duplicate payments processed under variations of the beneficiary's name, especially due to inconsistencies in transliteration (e.g., Gujarati to English).

### 1.2 Core System Objectives
*   **Proactive Anomaly Detection:** Shift from post-audit discovery to near real-time flagging of suspicious transactions.
*   **High-Performance Processing:** Capable of ingesting and evaluating over 10,000 transactions in under 30 seconds to support high-volume payment cycles.
*   **Explainable AI/Logic:** Provide a quantifiable, transparent risk score for every flagged transaction, accompanied by specific evidentiary citations (e.g., "95% probability of deceased status based on Civil Death Register match").
*   **Actionable Intelligence:** Transform raw data dumps into a prioritized, structured investigation queue for administrative officers.
*   **Closed-Loop Resolution:** Enable end-to-end tracking of flagged anomalies, from initial detection to field verification via GPS-stamped reporting.

## 2. User Roles and Responsibilities

The system is designed with a role-based access control (RBAC) architecture to cater to the specific operational needs of various stakeholders involved in the DBT ecosystem.

### 2.1 District Finance Officer (DFO)
The primary administrative authority at the district level responsible for financial integrity.
*   **Access Level:** District-wide data and investigation workflows.
*   **Key Responsibilities:**
    *   Monitor the prioritized investigation queue generated by the system.
    *   Review high-risk transactions and their associated evidence.
    *   Assign specific cases to Scheme Verifiers for on-ground field investigation.
    *   Review verification reports submitted by field staff.
    *   Approve or halt secondary disbursements based on investigation outcomes.
    *   Export structured audit reports for state-level compliance and record-keeping.

### 2.2 Scheme Verifier (Field Investigator)
The on-ground personnel responsible for physical verification of flagged beneficiaries.
*   **Access Level:** Assigned cases only (Mobile/Tablet interface optimized).
*   **Key Responsibilities:**
    *   Receive and review case assignments from the DFO.
    *   Navigate to the beneficiary's registered address for physical verification.
    *   Conduct interviews and verify documents (e.g., Aadhaar, Bank Passbook, Death Certificate if applicable).
    *   Submit verification findings directly into the system, strictly utilizing GPS-stamped submissions to ensure physical presence at the designated location.
    *   Upload supporting photographic evidence or documentation.

### 2.3 Audit Team Member
The compliance and oversight personnel responsible for systemic integrity and cross-scheme analysis.
*   **Access Level:** Read-only access to all transactional data, audit logs, and systemic flags across all districts.
*   **Key Responsibilities:**
    *   Query the database specifically for cross-scheme duplicate flags (e.g., a beneficiary claiming mutual exclusive benefits).
    *   Evaluate the accuracy of the system's risk scoring mechanisms over time.
    *   Generate comprehensive compliance summaries detailing the volume of prevented leakages versus false positives.
    *   Ensure adherence to state financial regulations.

### 2.4 State DBT Administrator
The central technical and policy authority managing the statewide DBT infrastructure.
*   **Access Level:** Super-admin privileges, system configuration, and state-wide analytics.
*   **Key Responsibilities:**
    *   Configure, tweak, and deploy leakage pattern rules and thresholds (e.g., adjusting the fuzzy matching threshold for transliterations).
    *   Monitor system health, performance metrics (processing times), and uptime.
    *   View the state-level risk heatmap, analyzing anomalies segmented by scheme, district, and leakage type.
    *   Integrate new data sources (e.g., updated Civil Death Registers, new scheme databases) into the ingestion pipeline.
